# Statistics Quiz — Answer Key

1. **A** — Statistics is defined in the lesson as the discipline concerned with the collection, organization, analysis, interpretation, and presentation of data.
2. **C** — A statistical population is a set of similar items of interest for some question or experiment; it is not limited to the accessible group, nor is it a sample or a single record.
3. **B** — A sample is a subset of the population that is *meant to represent* it — representativeness, not size or storage, is the defining feature.
4. **C** — The sampling frame is the source material or device from which the sample is drawn; the broader group is the population and the people who participate are the sample itself.
5. **A** — The funnel narrows from Population → Target Population → Accessible Population → Sample → Subject.
6. **D** — Sampling is preferred because measuring an entire population is often infeasible (cost, time, or impossibility, e.g., sizing every star); samples are not automatically more accurate.
7. **A** — Over-coverage is the inclusion of data from outside the population; under-coverage is the opposite (missing elements that do belong).
8. **C** — Listing clusters (households) instead of individuals is exactly the *grouping* error described in the lesson.
9. **B** — The lesson explicitly states that representativeness lies outside statistical theory and requires the judgment of subject-matter experts.
10. **C** — Inferential statistics is defined in the lesson as drawing conclusions from a sample onto the population; descriptive statistics only summarizes the group at hand.
11. **B** — Overlapping distributions support *generalizations* (e.g., "men are taller than women, on average") but rule out definitive positive or negative claims about every individual.
12. **D** — Histograms are explicitly listed as the tool for asking about range, center, skewness, bimodality, outliers, and subset variation.
13. **B** — A distribution is described by central tendency, dispersion, and skewness; mean/median/mode are only the first property, and range/variance/std are only the second.
14. **C** — The physical metaphor in the lesson treats the mean as the *center of mass* of the distribution.
15. **B** — Variance is the *average of the squared differences* from the mean, $\sigma^2 = \tfrac{1}{N}\sum (x_i - \mu)^2$, not the absolute deviation or the range.
16. **A** — Standard deviation is defined as the square root of the variance ($\sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2}$).
17. **C** — Larger variance means the mass is more spread out around the center; the center itself does not shift, and the distribution does not necessarily become more symmetric.
18. **A** — The 25th and 75th percentiles are also called the first and third quartiles (Q1 and Q3), or the lower and upper quartiles.
19. **B** — A positive `.skew()` corresponds to a right-skewed distribution, with the long tail on the *right* (higher values); the peak is actually on the *left* in that case.
20. **A** — The lesson states the mode is the peak, the mean pulls the curve toward the tail, and the median follows the mean part of the way, lying between mode and mean.
21. **A** — The Normal (Gaussian) Distribution is bell-shaped and symmetric around its mean, with the highest density at the center.
22. **C** — The Empirical Rule says roughly 68.2% within $1\sigma$, 95.4% within $2\sigma$, and 99.7% within $3\sigma$.
23. **B** — The rule is "empirical" because it is derived from repeated real-world observations, not from pure mathematical derivation; it shows up in vital signs, biology, test scores, and manufacturing tolerances.
24. **A** — The NBA example illustrates that an outlier in one distribution may actually be a member of another distribution (e.g., a 195 cm person fits within $3\sigma$ of NBA players' heights).
25. **D** — When a distribution is skewed, the median is recommended as the "typical" summary because the mean is overly sensitive to extreme outliers.
