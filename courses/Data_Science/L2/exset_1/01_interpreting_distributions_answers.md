## Exercise 1 — Comparative Sex IQ Distribution

1. **C** — Both curves peak at the dashed line marked μ = 100; centers align even though spread differs.
2. **B** — Men’s σ ≈ 16.2 yields a wider, flatter blue curve; women’s σ ≈ 13.2 is narrower and taller.
3. **C** — For probability densities, a narrower distribution must have a higher peak so total area remains 1.
4. **B** — The wider (men’s) distribution places more density in both low and high tails away from 100.
5. **A, C** — Both are roughly normal/symmetric; same mean does **not** imply identical distributions when σ differs (B and D are false).

## Exercise 4 — DataSaudi: Model Observations

Answers will vary in wording. Below are **acceptable model observations** grounded in DataSaudi’s published descriptions and typical chart readings. Students should cite what they **see** on the live site; numbers may update when GASTAT refreshes data.

### Plot A — Population Pyramid

**Overall shape**

- Pyramid / age-structure chart: many people at younger ages, tapering toward older ages — a relatively **young** population base with thinner bars at high ages.
- Not symmetric left–right: the chart compares **males vs. females** by age, not a single unimodal “age” density.

**By gender**

- **Females** often concentrate in younger childhood bands (site text cites peak around **5–9 years** for females in 2024).
- **Males** often show a large concentration in **working-age** bands (site text cites peak around **30–34 years** for males in 2024) — a pronounced **bulge** on the male side.
- Kingdom total population is **male-heavy** in recent summaries (e.g., ~62% male / ~38% female in 2024 overview text).

**Spread / tails**

- Very old age groups (80+) are **thin** — few people compared to young and working-age groups.
- **Asymmetry** between sides: male side wider in mid-adult ages; female side relatively wider at young ages depending on year/filter.

**Interpretation (example)**

- Large working-age male share is consistent with a labor-importing economy and expatriate workforce; broad youth base suggests future growth in working-age population if age structure persists.

### Plot B — Births and Newborns

**Center / mode**

- Births peak at mothers aged **25–29** (highest bar in the age-group distribution for Saudi mothers in recent GASTAT reporting on the site).
- **Mode** (most common age group) is not the same as **mean** age of mothers unless the distribution is symmetric — here the peak is a single age band, not necessarily the arithmetic average.

**Shape**

- Distribution is **unimodal** with a **right-skewed** tail: fewer births at very young mothers and a declining but non-zero tail into **older** age groups (30s and beyond).
- Not symmetric: long tail toward older ages, steep rise from teens into mid-20s.

**Spread**

- Most births fall in a **mid-range** of maternal ages (roughly early 20s through early 30s); very young and very old maternal age groups contribute **fewer** births.

**Comparison to Plot A**

- Pyramid: **stock** of people alive by **age and sex** at a point in time.
- Births chart: **flow** of newborns by **mother’s age** in a given year — one demographic rate, not the whole population structure.
- Different x-axis meaning (age of person vs. age of mother) and different question (who lives here vs. when do births happen).
