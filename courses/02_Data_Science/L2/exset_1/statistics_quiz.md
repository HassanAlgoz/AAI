# Statistics Quiz

Select the single best answer for each question.

---

1. Statistics, as a discipline, is most accurately defined by which set of activities applied to data?
   - A) Collecting, organizing, analyzing, interpreting, and presenting it.
   - B) Predicting future events from probabilistic computer simulations.
   - C) Storing, indexing, and querying it at industrial scale.
   - D) Visualizing trends to support marketing communications.

2. A *statistical population* is best described as:
   - A) the largest group a researcher can practically access during a study.
   - B) any subset deliberately drawn to mirror a broader group of interest.
   - C) a set of similar items that are of interest for a specific question.
   - D) the list of attributes recorded for one individual respondent.

3. A *sample* is best characterized as:
   - A) the full sampling frame from which observations are later drawn.
   - B) a subset of the population that is intended to represent the whole.
   - C) the accessible population that a researcher is able to recruit.
   - D) a duplicate copy of the population stored for archival purposes.

4. The *sampling frame* refers to:
   - A) the broad group that the study's conclusions are meant to describe.
   - B) the final set of individuals who actually take part in the study.
   - C) the source material or method from which a sample is drawn.
   - D) the statistical formulas used to estimate population parameters.

5. The lesson's "funnel" arranges these levels from broadest to narrowest in which order?
   - A) Population, Target Population, Accessible Population, Sample, Subject.
   - B) Population, Sample, Subject, Target Population, Accessible Population.
   - C) Target Population, Population, Sample, Accessible Population, Subject.
   - D) Sample, Subject, Accessible Population, Target Population, Population.

6. The principal reason researchers prefer sampling over recording the whole population is that:
   - A) samples usually yield more accurate estimates than full censuses do.
   - B) statistical theory cannot be applied to fully enumerated populations.
   - C) sampling removes the need for judgment about representativeness.
   - D) full enumeration is often infeasible due to cost or accessibility.

7. Including responses from individuals who fall outside the target population illustrates:
   - A) over-coverage in the sampling frame.
   - B) under-coverage in the sampling frame.
   - C) processing error in the data pipeline.
   - D) measurement error in the questionnaire.

8. A frame that lists households instead of the individuals living in them illustrates the error of:
   - A) duplicate entries within the sampling frame.
   - B) participation bias among the selected individuals.
   - C) grouping of clusters in place of individuals.
   - D) measurement error during the data collection phase.

9. According to the lesson, deciding whether a sampling frame is representative of the population is:
   - A) a question fully resolved by formal statistical theory.
   - B) primarily a matter of expert judgment in the subject area.
   - C) a task that is settled purely by increasing the sample size.
   - D) a step that the sampling software typically handles automatically.

10. Drawing conclusions from a sample about the population it came from is the role of:
    - A) descriptive statistics applied numerically to the sample.
    - B) descriptive statistics applied visually to the sample.
    - C) inferential statistics applied to the sample data.
    - D) sampling frame construction during the preprocessing stage.

11. According to the lesson, when two distributions visibly overlap, statistics still allows us to make:
    - A) definitive negative claims such as "no member of A exceeds any member of B."
    - B) general claims that hold on average, but not absolute claims about every case.
    - C) absolute claims about every individual within either group.
    - D) no useful claims at all until the overlap is fully removed.

12. A histogram is most directly useful for visually answering which combination of questions?
    - A) Average daily revenue and quarterly growth of a product line.
    - B) Correlation, causation, and the direction of an observed effect.
    - C) Sampling errors and the probability of non-response bias.
    - D) Range, center, skewness, bimodality, and presence of outliers.

13. The lesson describes a distribution by which three properties?
    - A) Sample size, sampling frame, and population coverage.
    - B) Central tendency, dispersion, and skewness.
    - C) Mean, median, and mode considered together as one bundle.
    - D) Histogram, percentile, and the empirical rule combined.

14. The lesson's physical metaphor presents the *mean* of a distribution as:
    - A) the peak of the curve drawn over the histogram bars.
    - B) the value that minimizes the total range of the data.
    - C) the center of mass that supports the distribution's weight.
    - D) the boundary between the lower and upper quartiles.

15. Variance ($\sigma^2$) is computed from a population of $N$ values by:
    - A) summing the absolute deviations from the mean and dividing by $N$.
    - B) averaging the squared differences between each value and the mean.
    - C) taking the square root of the average distance from the median.
    - D) dividing the data's range by the number of observations.

16. The standard deviation ($\sigma$) is best described as:
    - A) the square root of the variance of the distribution.
    - B) the average squared distance from the central tendency.
    - C) the difference between the maximum and minimum values.
    - D) the gap between the first and third percentiles of the data.

17. In the lesson's physical metaphor, an *increase* in variance corresponds to:
    - A) the center of mass shifting toward one tail of the distribution.
    - B) the mass becoming more concentrated at a single point.
    - C) the mass becoming more spread out around the center.
    - D) the curve becoming more symmetric around its average value.

18. The 25% and 75% percentiles together are also known as:
    - A) the first and third quartiles, or Q1 and Q3.
    - B) the lower and upper bounds of the standard deviation.
    - C) the median and mode of the central tendency.
    - D) the inner and outer fences of the empirical rule.

19. A distribution whose `.skew()` method returns a clearly positive value is best described as:
    - A) symmetric, with the peak located precisely at the dataset's mean.
    - B) right-skewed, with a long tail extending toward higher values.
    - C) left-skewed, with the peak shifted toward the higher values.
    - D) bimodal, with two peaks of equal height at opposite ends.

20. According to the lesson, when a distribution is skewed, the positions of its *mode*, *median*, and *mean* satisfy:
    - A) the mode sits at the peak, the mean is pulled toward the tail, and the median lies between them.
    - B) the mean sits at the peak, while the mode and median both lag equally at the tail.
    - C) all three coincide at exactly one shared point along the data axis.
    - D) the median sits at the peak, with the mean and mode shifted to opposite tails.

21. The Normal Distribution is most accurately described as a curve that is:
    - A) bell-shaped and symmetric around its mean.
    - B) flat-topped and identical at every value of the variable.
    - C) right-skewed, with most density at the lower end of the range.
    - D) bimodal, with two equally tall peaks separated by a low middle.

22. According to the Empirical Rule for a Normal Distribution, the percentage of data falling within two standard deviations of the mean is closest to:
    - A) 50.0%.
    - B) 68.2%.
    - C) 95.4%.
    - D) 99.7%.

23. The Empirical Rule is called "empirical" because:
    - A) it can be derived from probability axioms without any reference to data.
    - B) it was discovered through repeated observations across many real datasets.
    - C) it applies exclusively to manufactured items rather than living systems.
    - D) it holds only when the sample size sits below a specific fixed threshold.

24. A 195 cm adult who looks like an outlier among adult men yet fits comfortably within the NBA players distribution most directly illustrates that:
    - A) an outlier within one distribution may belong to a different distribution.
    - B) outliers should always be discarded before any further analysis is done.
    - C) NBA players form a normal distribution while adult men generally do not.
    - D) the standard deviation becomes irrelevant when comparing two distributions.

25. When a distribution is strongly skewed by extreme outliers, the lesson recommends summarizing a "typical" value using the:
    - A) mean, since it incorporates every single observation in the data.
    - B) range, since it captures the full breadth of the distribution.
    - C) mode, since it always equals the most representative value present.
    - D) median, since it is far less sensitive to extreme outliers.
