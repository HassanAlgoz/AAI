# AI Policy

Recent research into the educational use of artificial intelligence has begun to document the cognitive and psychological consequences of relying on generative models. While AI can process data and organize information rapidly, studies indicate that **using it as a substitute for active thinking can lead to cognitive atrophy, diminished critical analysis, and reduced academic engagement**.[^Kosmyna2025][^Gerlich2025][^Benedek2025]

## Recent studies: detrimental effects of AI on learning

### **1. The Accumulation of "Cognitive Debt"**

**Source:** MIT Media Lab — Kosmyna et al. (2025)[^Kosmyna2025]
Researchers observed the brain activity of individuals writing essays under three conditions: using ChatGPT, using a standard search engine, and using no tools at all.

* **Reduced Neural Engagement:** Using EEG tracking, the study found that the ChatGPT group exhibited the lowest brain engagement and the weakest neural connectivity, particularly in the brain waves associated with memory, semantic processing, and creative ideation.
* **Poor Memory Retention:** When participants were later asked to rewrite their essays without assistance, the AI group remembered very little of their work. The AI bypassed their deep memory networks because the participants did not actively struggle with the material.
* **Homogenized Output:** Essays generated with AI were largely identical in structure and phrasing. Evaluators described them as "soulless" and lacking original thought.

### **2. Decreasing Knowledge and the "Law of Less Work"**

**Source:** Operations Research course experiment — Benedek & Sziklai (2025)[^Benedek2025]
An experiment conducted in a university course divided students into two groups: one permitted to use AI during classes and exams, and a control group required to work entirely offline.

* **Lower Baseline Understanding:** The AI-permitted group scored significantly lower on basic offline true-or-false assessments and subsequent midterms compared to the offline group.
* **Superficial Engagement:** The researchers noted that unrestricted access to AI caused students to fall victim to the "Law of Less Work." Because the AI could easily generate passing outputs, students ignored foundational concepts and completely failed to grasp the core material.

### **3. Cognitive Offloading and Critical Thinking Decline**

**Source:** *AI Tools in Society* — Gerlich (2025)[^Gerlich2025]
This research focused on "cognitive offloading"—the act of delegating mental tasks to external aids to reduce the load on one's working memory.

* **Bypassing Analytical Skills:** The study revealed a significant negative correlation between frequent AI usage and critical thinking abilities. Because AI offers immediate, ready-made solutions, it discourages the slow, reflective cognitive processes required to develop independent problem-solving skills. Younger participants exhibited the highest dependence on the tools and the lowest critical thinking scores.

### **4. Loss of Interpersonal Connection and Well-being**

**Source:** Center for Democracy & Technology (2025)[^CDT2025]
Beyond cognitive impacts, surveys of K-12 and higher education environments highlight the social and psychological costs of AI integration.

* **Weakened Relationships:** Half of the students surveyed reported that using AI in class made them feel less connected to their teachers. Parents and educators also noted a decrease in peer-to-peer collaboration.
* **Erosion of Intrinsic Motivation:** Studies exploring student well-being note that the ease of generating answers can create complacency. When students are eventually required to complete tasks independently, they often experience a heightened sense of inadequacy and anxiety, which erodes their intrinsic motivation and academic self-efficacy over time.

---

### **The Core Consensus**

Learning only occurs when the brain is actively engaged in making sense of information. The prevailing conclusion across these studies is that when AI is used to *produce an output* rather than *assist in a process*, it undercuts learning.[^Zhai2024][^Shah2026][^Macnamara2024]

> If AI does the thinking, the student might get the grade, but they lose the cognitive development that the assignment was designed to foster.

---

## Using AI Without Losing the Learning

The same body of research points to conditions under which AI **supports** deep learning, creativity, and higher-order skills rather than shortcutting them.[^Zhai2024][^Bai2023]

### Habits and Conditions to Aim For

1. Use in guided and time-limited bursts — shorter, structured use shows strong gains and less erosion of engagement than long, unguided sessions.[^Xu2024][^Liu2025]
2. Try first without AI, then consult it to compare, refine, or check; periodically test yourself without AI to monitor real skill growth.[^Vieriu2025][^Zhai2024][^Shah2026][^Macnamara2024][^Ahn2024]
3. Treat it as practice and exploration ("critique this answer", "give me counter-arguments"), not a way to generate the whole product.[^Vieriu2025][^Zhai2024][^Nabiyeva2025][^Ahn2024][^Bauer2025]
4. Use AI mainly to explain, scaffold, quiz, simulate, or give feedback — while you still plan, solve, and write yourself.[^Tlili2025][^Adewale2024][^Zhai2024][^Bai2023]
5. Knowing what AI is good at and what it is bad at, and apply its output selectively.[^Msambwa2025][^Ahn2024]

### Good and Bad Environments

Bad: environments **optimizing for speed and grades**, with weak emphasis on process, reasoning, and originality.[^GarciaMartinez2023][^Msambwa2025][^Shanmugasundaram2023][^Nabiyeva2025][^Bauer2025]

Good: **Demands critical use** — you critique, verify, or extend its answers, and blend it with conventional study and discussion.[^Vieriu2025][^Zhai2024][^Nabiyeva2025][^Ahn2024]

## Conclusion

Good use of AI means it **augments, rather than replaces, thinking** — used for feedback, hints, explanations, practice, or extra resources, while you still do the core reasoning, writing, and problem-solving.[^Tlili2025][^Adewale2024][^Zhai2024][^Bai2023][^Nabiyeva2025][^Ahn2024][^Bauer2025]
