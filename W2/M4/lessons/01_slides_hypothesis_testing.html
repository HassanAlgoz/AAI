Hypothesis Testing

Descriptive vs inferential

  • Describe: means, plots, tables for the data in hand.
  • Infer: use those summaries to reason about what we did not observe—under uncertainty.

We make claims and test them with defined procedures.

Frame the notebook’s arc: we already know how to summarize samples; now we ask whether patterns are more than noise. Tie to the course story (e.g., “Are NBA players really taller?”): that is inference, not just plotting means.

Walk the diagram: descriptive stays with the sample; inference reaches toward the population or toward “if we repeated the study.” The external image in the notebook serves the same role; this local asset matches the idea. Emphasize: inference always involves assumptions and limits—we will name a few (normality, independence) later.

Everyday claims

People assert things like:

  • Coffee keeps you awake at night.
  • Exercise makes you smarter.
  • “Everyone” is addicted to smartphones.

Question: how do we test such statements against reality—not with one story, but with data?

Use this slide to surface anecdotes vs systematic evidence. Do not dismiss anecdotes; they generate hypotheses—but they do not replace sampling and tests. Transition: we formalize claims as \(H_0\) and \(H_a\).

Two competing statements

In every formal test we line up:

  1. Null hypothesis (\(H_0\)): the “status quo” or no effect. Any difference you see could be chance (sampling noise).
  2. Alternative hypothesis (\(H_a\) or \(H_1\)): what you seek evidence for—a real effect, difference, or relationship.

Stress language: we never “prove \(H_0\)” in a single study; we fail to reject it when evidence is weak. That sets up the p-value slide. Students often confuse “accept null” with “no effect”—correct that gently.

Why anecdotes fail

Anecdotal evidence: personal stories or isolated incidents—not a systematic sample.

We turn anecdotes into parameters and data:

  • Swordfish mercury → compare mean mercury in many fish to a regulatory threshold.
  • Duke graduation time → compare Duke’s distribution to a national benchmark.
  • A new drug → compare survival in treatment vs control (placebo), not one patient.

Use the notebook’s three vignettes quickly: news story, two students at Duke, friend’s dad. For each, name the population parameter (mean \(\mu\), proportion \(p\), survival rate) and the need for comparison groups where relevant.

Which test answers your question?

Methods differ by what you ask:

  1. Difference in one numeric variable (often t-tests).
  2. Association between two categorical variables (chi-square).
  3. Association between two numeric variables (Pearson correlation—and alternatives).

Keep it high level: this deck focuses on t-tests; chi-square and Pearson are pointers to later work or other modules. The decision tree slide will show how these pieces fit on one map.

p-values and \(\alpha\)

Test output includes a p-value.

  • If p \(< \alpha\) (convention: \(\alpha = 0.05\)), we reject \(H_0\).
  • If p \(\ge \alpha\), we fail to reject \(H_0\)—not the same as “\(H_0\) is true.”

Courtroom analogy: \(H_0\) \(\approx\) “innocent until proven guilty.” “Not guilty” can mean lack of evidence, not proof of innocence.

Hammer the wording: “fail to reject” not “accept”. Same as the notebook. If time, mention that \(\alpha\) is a community convention, not a law of nature—preregistered studies may justify other thresholds.

Testing differences: three t-test situations

t-tests compare means in three setups:

  1. One sample: sample mean vs a reference (population or standard).
  2. Independent samples: two unrelated groups (e.g., treatment vs control).
  3. Paired: same units twice, or natural pairs (twins, matched pairs).

Case 1: One sample vs a reference

Compare \(\bar{x}\) from your sample to a known or assumed \(\mu\):

  • Student heights vs national average.
  • IQ in a group vs population mean.
  • Cholesterol vs clinical guideline.

Notebook example: NBA player heights vs general population ( ttest_1samp ).

“Population” here often means a benchmark from literature or policy, not a full census. The NBA vs humans example is vivid: huge separation, tiny p-value—good for pedagogy, not subtle statistics.

Case 2: Two independent samples

Two different groups:

  • Control (placebo) vs treatment (drug).
  • Male vs female penguins on body mass (ttest_ind).

Notebook: tips from smokers vs non-smokers (same function, different story).

Check independence: different rows, no pairing. Common mistake: using ttest_ind on before/after data from the same people—that is paired.

Case 3: Paired data

3.1 One group, two measurements: before/after, method A vs method B on the same subjects (spinach and grip strength in the notebook).

3.2 Natural pairs: twin A vs twin B, husband–wife, mother–baby—each row is a pair (ttest_rel).

Contrast with independent: pairing removes between-person noise and can hugely change the result. The notebook’s grip-strength example is ideal for code walkthrough in the notebook itself.

Beyond two groups: ANOVA (preview)

  • A t-test compares two means.
  • With three or more groups (e.g., penguin species on body mass) use one-way ANOVA as the omnibus test; or better yet: Tukey HSD.
