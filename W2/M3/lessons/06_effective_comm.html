Effective communication of exploratory results

Why this matters

Graphs communicate ideas quickly—but images can also mislead.

This deck covers best practices for presenting data accurately and clearly. Visualization has room for creativity, yet solid principles still apply.

Open with the tension: a picture can be worth a thousand words, but it can also lie or mislead. The goal of this session is not to kill creativity—visualization is partly an art—but to give you guiding principles so your exploratory graphics present data accurately and effectively. Everything that follows supports that single aim.

What’s wrong with this plot?

Pause on the image before you explain. Ask the room what feels off—scale, encoding, comparison, or story. 1. Keep it simple

Decide what you want the reader to see. Use color to group or differentiate levels meaningfully—not only to brighten the plot.

Pie slices are hard to compare; arbitrary colors distract; 3D rarely helps. A bar chart usually makes category magnitudes easier to read, especially with more than a few levels.

Use the classic manufacturing cost example mentally: five cost categories. On the pie, ask aloud whether your eye can really compare “Buildings and administration” to “Workplace materials”—slice angles are poor for magnitude. Call out three problems: hard comparisons, meaningless rainbow-like colors, and 3D that adds no insight. Then contrast with the bar chart: same categories, but length is easy to judge. Emphasize the rule: for more than a few categories, bars usually beat pies for reading values.

2. Use color to draw attention

Add color on purpose: draw attention to what matters, and question defaults (e.g., do red and purple bars imply a relationship?).

Not everyone perceives color the same way—pair color with another cue (e.g., pattern) when you need to refer to highlighted elements in more than one way.

Beauty is fine, but defaults are a decision—often a bad one. Walk through (a) vs (b): in (b), color points to one message; in (a), redundant hues make viewers wonder if red and purple “mean” something related. Mention accessibility: color vision varies; panel (c) shows pairing color with pattern so you can refer to the highlighted category in two ways. This is a good place to say “if you only remember one thing about color: use it to direct attention, not to decorate.”

3. Tell a story

Often the plot should include context that is not in the raw table—annotations, reference lines, or events that frame the narrative.

Example: mark the start of a fiscal year. A diagonal line (y = x) can separate points where one coordinate exceeds the other.

The dataset rarely contains the whole story. External information—policy dates, fiscal year starts, experimental conditions—belongs on the plot when it explains why the series looks the way it does. The text’s example: annotating the start of the university fiscal year. For scatterplots, a (y = x) line is standard: above the line, one variable exceeds the other; below, the reverse. That line is “context” encoded as geometry. Encourage students to ask: “What would a domain expert need to see that isn’t in the CSV?”

4. Order matters

Software may order categories alphabetically by default—that is not always meaningful.

Consider ordering by magnitude or by the natural sequence of the question (e.g., survey response order). A misleading order can fool a quick scan of the axes.

Most tools alphabetize or use arbitrary order unless you override. Explain that alphabetical order is often meaningless for opinion or severity scales—readers skim the axis and infer a trend that isn’t there. Offer two better options: sort by count (tallest to shortest) when the story is “who is biggest,” and survey or logical order when the scale itself is ordinal (e.g., “very well” through “very badly”). Stress: wrong order misleads even when the numbers are correct. The next slide gives a real survey where question order is the right order.

5. Make labels easy to read

Same numbers, different layout: horizontal bars often leave more room for category labels than vertical bars.

Clear labels → more learning from the chart. Aim for succinct, readable text.

This continues the Brexit theme: results by region in stacked bars. (a) vertical vs (b) horizontal—the values are identical; only layout changes. Horizontal bars buy space for long region names on the axis. Repeat the mantra: the easier the labels, the more the reader actually reads. If something is hard to read, they skip it and your chart under-delivers. Tie to the chapter goal: maximum information, clearly, not maximum ink.

6. Pick a purpose

Every graphical choice should serve the research question. Defaults are not always the right story.

Stacked bars emphasize one kind of comparison; faceting or alternative encodings may fit another. Use informative labels, titles, and sources when you can.

Stacked bars answer “what is the composition within each group?”—not always the question you care about. Same data, three stories: (a) if proportions across regions are the headline, stacked or normalized views help. (b) if counts matter—not just shares—show totals or axes that preserve magnitude. (c) faceting—one small multiple per region—when you want readers to compare distributions within region without stacking confusion. Mention that good figures also carry titles and a data source URL; the book highlights that on (c). Preview the next slide: more variants of the same idea.

Same data, different emphasis

Briefly contrast this panel with the previous: reinforce that encoding choice = editorial choice. Ask: “For your analysis, is the story about share, about total (N), or about per-region detail?” There is no single “correct” chart—only a chart that matches the question. Remind them to strip redundant ink and to label what matters.

7. Select meaningful colors

Avoid rainbow defaults when they do not match your variable. Choose palettes that are distinct and work for color-vision diversity (e.g., cividis for ordinal data—Nuñez, Anderton, and Renslow 2018).

Rainbow scales are popular in defaults but often perceptually uneven—equal steps in data don’t look equal in hue. Research-backed palettes (the slide mentions cividis for ordinal data) aim for discriminability and robustness across common forms of color-vision deficiency. Same plot, two themes: let the audience see how “prettier” is not the same as “more informative.” Point to the paper if someone teaches or publishes in this space.

Takeaway

Your graph should carry maximum information in minimum ink: clear labels (often horizontal), strong titles, and data source when possible.

One-sentence synthesis of the chapter: maximal information, succinctly and clearly—Tufte’s spirit without naming him if you prefer. Horizontal labels when you can, titles that state the claim, and sources so the figure is trustworthy. This is a good moment to loop back to the opening “bad plot”: ask which principles it violated. End upbeat: these habits become automatic with practice.
