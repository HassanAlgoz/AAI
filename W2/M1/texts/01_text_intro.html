    The Invisible Threat

    In the late summer of 1854, the Soho district of London experienced a devastating outbreak of cholera. Within a few days, hundreds of people died. At the time, the prevailing medical consensus was the “miasma theory”—the idea that diseases were spread by foul-smelling, poisoned air emanating from decaying organic matter.

    Because Soho was crowded and had poor sanitation, the miasma theory seemed to fit. The government’s public health interventions focused on trying to clean up the smell. However, one physician, John Snow, approached the problem differently. He did not accept the established consensus because it lacked rigorous empirical support. He suspected cholera was caused by something ingested, specifically through contaminated water.

    Gathering the Data

    Snow could not see the cholera bacteria; the microscope technology of the time was not advanced enough to easily identify it, and the germ theory of disease was not yet established. Instead of arguing theory, Snow gathered data.

    He walked the streets of Soho, knocking on doors and interviewing the families of those who had died. He recorded two critical pieces of information for every household:

    1. The location of the home.
    2. The source of their drinking water.

    He then took this tabular data and visualized it. He took a street map of Soho and drew a black bar for every cholera death at the exact address where it occurred.

    Identifying the Pattern and Testing Anomalies

    When the data was plotted spatially, a clear pattern emerged. The deaths were not spread evenly through the “bad air” of Soho. They were heavily concentrated in a specific cluster around the public water pump on Broad Street.

    In data analysis, identifying a trend is only the first step; a robust model must also account for outliers and anomalies. Snow looked closely at the exceptions to his spatial pattern:

    • The Brewery: There was a large brewery located very close to the Broad Street pump, yet none of the workers had contracted cholera. Snow investigated and found that the workers were given a daily allowance of beer and rarely drank from the public pump. Furthermore, the brewery had its own private deep well.
    • The Workhouse: A nearby workhouse surrounded by cholera cases had almost no deaths. Snow found they also had their own private well.
    • The Hampstead Widow: Snow found a record of a widow who died of cholera miles away in Hampstead, an area entirely free of the outbreak. He visited her home and discovered that she used to live in Soho and liked the taste of the Broad Street water so much that she had a servant fetch a large bottle of it every week. Her niece, who visited and drank the water, also died.

    These anomalies, rather than breaking his hypothesis, provided the strongest evidence for it. The variable controlling the disease was not proximity to bad air, but the specific consumption of water from the Broad Street pump.

    Data to Action

    Snow presented his map and his findings to the local Board of Guardians. The evidence was clear enough to prompt an immediate, physical intervention.

    “I had an interview with the Board of Guardians of St. James’s parish… In consequence of what I said, the handle of the pump was removed on the following day.” — John Snow

    The removal of the pump handle stopped the immediate outbreak. Later investigations revealed that the Broad Street well had been dug mere feet from a leaking cesspit.

    Why This Matters for Data Science

    John Snow’s work perfectly encapsulates the core process of data science, long before computers existed:

    1. Questioning Assumptions: He did not accept the “miasma” consensus because it was not supported by evidence.
    2. Data Collection: He systematically gathered raw, verifiable information (locations and water sources).
    3. Visualization: He mapped the data to reveal spatial patterns that were invisible in a simple list of numbers.
    4. Investigating Outliers: He rigorously checked his hypothesis against the exceptions (the brewery, the widow).
    5. Actionable Insight: He translated his findings into a specific, targeted intervention (removing the pump handle) that solved the problem.

    Data Science is not merely about writing code or knowing complex equations. It is about using evidence to uncover the truth of a situation and making informed decisions. Throughout this course, you will learn the modern tools to do exactly what John Snow did: gather data, find the pattern, explain the anomalies, and remove the pump handle.

    What is Data Science?

    Data (plural) is a digitally recorded small piece of information about an event. When put together, one can piece together the full picture. But, first, we store them on computers.

    • Data can be numbers, text, images, sounds, etc.
    • Data also falls into types: numerical, categorical and sub-types.
    • Data is analyzed using different algorithms for different purposes.

    .. there is alot to say about data .. hence, we have Data Science.

    Data Science is an interdisciplinary field that uses scientific methods, processes, algorithms, and systems to extract knowledge and insights from structured and unstructured data. It combines principles and techniques from statistics, computer science, and domain-specific knowledge to analyze, interpret, and leverage data for decision-making and predictive analytics.

    • Domain Knowledge is essential for asking the right questions and for understanding the answers produced by computational tools.
    • Math & Statistics studies how to make robust conclusions based on incomplete information.
    • Computer Science is needed since data are stored on computers and processed by algorithms.

    How did John Snow do it?

    • Domain Knowledge: Snow is a doctor
    • Math & Statistics: Snow used data, plotted it, and drew conclusions
    • Computer Science: Snow did not have computers, but the data was stored on paper, and he did not need to do much computations.

    The use of computers does not give us more intelligence, it simply gives us the ability to get data in real-time from users of our systems. What we do with this data needs our own analytical mindset and purpose.

    Data Science Life Cycle

    This image illustrates the standard Data Science Life Cycle (the sequence of steps required to turn raw data into useful tools or insights) and maps those steps to the specific job titles responsible for them.

    1. The Process

    This represents the sequential journey of data from its raw state to a finished product.

    1. Collection: This is the act of gathering raw data from various sources. It involves extracting records from databases, logging user actions on a website, or downloading files.
    2. Cleaning: Raw data is rarely ready to use. It contains missing values, duplicates, and errors. Cleaning is the process of standardizing formats and fixing or removing corrupted data so that it doesn’t skew the final results.
    3. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA): Before building complex systems, you must understand what the data looks like. This involves calculating basic statistics (averages, ranges) and creating visual charts to find trends, correlations, or anomalies. 4. Model Building: This is where machine learning happens. It involves feeding the cleaned, understood data into mathematical algorithms so the computer can learn to recognize patterns and make predictions on new, unseen data.
    4. Model Deployment: A predictive model is useless if it only sits on a researcher’s laptop. Deployment is the software engineering process of integrating that model into a live application, website, or server where it can process new data in real-time.

    2. The Roles

    Different specialists focus on different parts of the pipeline.

    • Data Engineers (Collection & Cleaning): These are essentially software engineers who build the underlying infrastructure. They construct the databases and automated pipelines that safely extract, store, and organize massive amounts of data so that others can use it.
    • Data Analysts (Cleaning & EDA): Analysts focus on understanding historical data to answer specific business questions. They take the data provided by the engineers, ensure it is clean for their specific needs, and build reports or dashboards to explain what has happened in the past.
    • Machine Learning Engineers (Model Building & Deployment): These professionals bridge the gap between mathematics and software engineering. They take a predictive concept and write the code to ensure it runs efficiently, accurately, and reliably in a live production environment.
    • Data Scientists (The Entire Pipeline): Data Scientists are the generalists of the field. As the long bottom bar shows, they possess the technical skills to handle the entire lifecycle from start to finish. In practice, they primarily focus heavily on EDA and Model Building, but they must understand data engineering to get their data, and deployment to ensure their models are usable.

    While this diagram cleanly separates these roles, the real world is often messier. In large technology companies, these divisions are strict. In smaller companies or startups, a single “Data Scientist” might be expected to do the jobs of all four roles simultaneously.
